# What is the Fair Chance Act?

The Fair Chance Act prohibits California employers with five or more employees from asking job candidates about conviction history before making a conditional job offer.

‚Ä¢Part of the California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA)

‚Ä¢Enforced by the California Civil Rights Department (CRD)

‚Ä¢Purpose: reduce employment barriers for individuals with criminal histories, improve community outcomes, and reduce recidivism

## Who Must Comply?
### The law applies to:

‚Ä¢Private employers with 5+ employees

‚Ä¢Public employers

‚Ä¢Staffing and temporary employment agencies

‚Ä¢Labor contractors and client employers

‚Ä¢Union hiring halls

### Exemptions:

‚Ä¢Employers with fewer than 5 employees

‚Ä¢Law enforcement and criminal justice agencies

‚Ä¢Jobs where state or federal law requires a criminal background check

# Pre-Offer Prohibition: Timing is Everything
### Under the Fair Chance Act:

You cannot ask about criminal history until after making a conditional job offer.

### Prohibited actions before a conditional offer:

‚Ä¢Questions on job applications about convictions

‚Ä¢Criminal history questions in interviews

‚Ä¢Conducting background checks

‚Ä¢Searching online/social media for criminal history

‚Ä¢Job postings with statements like ‚Äúclean record required‚Äù

Conditional job offer: The candidate is selected for the position, pending completion of background checks or other conditions. Hiring decisions must prioritize skills and qualifications first.

# Criminal History That Cannot Be Used
### Even after a conditional offer, certain records cannot influence hiring decisions:

‚Ä¢Arrests without conviction

‚Ä¢Participation in diversion programs

‚Ä¢Expunged, sealed, or dismissed convictions

‚Ä¢Juvenile adjudications

‚Ä¢Marijuana-related convictions older than 2 years (non-felony)

Important: If a candidate‚Äôs background check reveals a conviction, you must conduct a thorough individualized assessment before rescinding the offer.

# Individualized Assessment Requirements
### Employers must carefully evaluate criminal history using three mandatory factors:

### 1. Nature and gravity of the offense
Consider context, severity, and whether circumstances like trauma, disability, or coercion contributed
### 2. Time elapsed since offense
Longer time since the offense generally reduces its relevance
Consider conduct and achievements since the conviction
### 3. Nature of the job
Evaluate whether the conviction directly relates to the specific duties of the position

## Evidence of Rehabilitation
### Applicants may voluntarily provide evidence of rehabilitation or mitigating circumstances:

‚Ä¢Employment history before/after the offense

‚Ä¢Education, training, or counseling programs

‚Ä¢Community service

‚Ä¢Letters of recommendation

‚Ä¢Circumstances contributing to the offense, including disability or trauma

Employers must consider any evidence voluntarily provided.

# Step-by-Step Process for Rescinding a Conditional Offer

1. Initial Individualized Assessment ‚Äì Document analysis of conviction using the three factors

2. Pre-Adverse Action Notice ‚Äì Provide written notice including convictions, reasoning, and 5 business days to respond

3. Five Business Days ‚Äì Candidate may challenge or provide mitigating evidence

4. Reassessment ‚Äì Review any new evidence before making a final decision

5. Final Notice ‚Äì Provide decision, procedure to challenge, and CRD filing rights
